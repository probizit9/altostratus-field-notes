What is Goral in Judaism?

Goral (גורל) is the Hebrew word for "lot" or "fate," referring to an ancient Jewish practice of seeking divine guidance through the casting or drawing of lots. This tradition appears throughout the Hebrew Bible and has been practiced in various forms throughout Jewish history, particularly within mystical and Kabbalistic contexts.

The concept of goral is deeply embedded in Jewish scripture. The most well-known biblical example occurs on Yom Kippur, when the High Priest would cast lots to determine which of two goats would be sent to Azazel (Leviticus 16:8). The Land of Israel was also divided among the tribes through goral (Numbers 26:55-56), and the prophet Jonah was identified through lots cast by sailors (Jonah 1:7).

Biblical and Historical Foundation

Unlike forms of divination explicitly forbidden in the Torah, goral was viewed as a legitimate way to discern God's will in specific circumstances. The practice assumes that when humans have exhausted their own understanding, they may turn to Heaven for guidance, allowing divine providence to reveal itself through seemingly random selection.

Key biblical instances of goral include:

  • The Yom Kippur Service: Two identical goats, with lots determining their destinies (Leviticus 16:8-10)
  • Tribal Land Distribution: The portions of Israel allocated by lot under Joshua (Numbers 26:55, Joshua 18-19)
  • Priestly Duties: Temple service assignments determined through goral (1 Chronicles 24:5)
  • Identifying Jonah: Sailors casting lots to find who brought misfortune (Jonah 1:7)
  • Purim: Haman casting pur (a form of lot) to choose the date to destroy the Jews (Esther 3:7)

Goral in Jewish Mystical Tradition

Within Kabbalah and Jewish mysticism, goral evolved beyond simple lot-casting into a more sophisticated practice of seeking guidance. Kabbalistic traditions developed methods of consulting sacred Hebrew texts—particularly verses from Tanakh (the Hebrew Bible)—as a way to receive answers to pressing life questions.

This practice, sometimes called She'elat Chalom (dream question) or various forms of bibliomancy using Jewish texts, involves focusing on a specific question and then opening to or selecting a passage that provides insight. The interpretation requires knowledge of Hebrew, familiarity with Jewish texts, and understanding of mystical hermeneutics.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does a goral reading work?

A goral reading involves seeking guidance through the tradition of sacred lots mentioned throughout the Hebrew Bible. During a live reading, a practitioner trained in Jewish mystical traditions interprets the goral in response to your specific question. The process typically involves focusing on your question, drawing or selecting from traditional Hebrew texts or symbols, and receiving an interpretation rooted in Kabbalistic wisdom and scriptural understanding.

Is goral reading kosher or permitted in Judaism?

Goral has biblical precedent and appears throughout Jewish texts, including the casting of lots for Yom Kippur (Leviticus 16:8), the division of the Land of Israel (Numbers 26:55), and the selection of Jonah (Jonah 1:7). Traditional Jewish sources distinguish between goral as seeking divine guidance and forbidden divination practices. Many religious authorities view goral as permissible when approached with proper intent and within appropriate contexts. Individual rabbis and communities may have varying perspectives on this practice.

What is the difference between goral and tarot?

Goral is rooted specifically in Jewish tradition and scripture, drawing from Hebrew texts, Kabbalistic wisdom, and the biblical practice of casting lots. Tarot originates from medieval European card games and developed into a divinatory system using symbolic imagery unconnected to Jewish sources. Goral readings reference Torah, Talmud, and Jewish mystical teachings, while tarot uses its own symbolic framework. For someone seeking guidance within a Jewish spiritual context, goral represents a tradition connected to their heritage rather than an external system.
