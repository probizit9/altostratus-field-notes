How do I reduce GPU costs for AI inference workloads?

If your AI company is spending $100,000 to $1,000,000+ monthly on GPU inference, systematic cost optimization typically reduces that bill by 40-60% without sacrificing performance. This requires understanding your workload characteristics, right-sizing infrastructure, and implementing continuous FinOps practices.

The Core Problem: Why GPU Inference Costs Spiral

Most AI companies overspend on inference GPUs for predictable reasons:

  • Over-provisioning for peak load: Capacity sits idle 60-80% of the time outside traffic spikes
  • Wrong GPU selection: Using H100s when L4s would deliver better cost-per-inference for your model size
  • Poor batching: Processing requests individually instead of dynamic batching with 5-20ms wait windows
  • No visibility: Teams lack per-endpoint, per-model cost tracking to identify optimization targets
  • Inertia: "It works" mindset prevents testing alternative providers, regions, or configurations

Practical GPU Cost Reduction Strategies

1. Establish Cost Visibility and Metrics

You cannot optimize what you don't measure. Implement tracking for:

  • Cost per 1M tokens (or per request) segmented by model and endpoint
  • GPU utilization (compute, memory, idle time) at 1-minute granularity
  • Request latency distribution (p50, p95, p99) tied to infrastructure costs
  • Batch size distribution and wait times in your serving queue

This baseline reveals where the largest inefficiencies exist. Most companies discover 30-40% of their GPU capacity runs below 20% utilization.

2. Right-Size GPU Instance Types

Match GPU selection to model requirements:

  • Models up to 7-13B parameters: L4, T4, or RTX 4090 instances deliver lowest cost-per-token. L4s on GCP or AWS often cost $0.50-1.00/hour versus $4-5/hour for A100s.
  • Models 30-70B parameters: A100 (40GB/80GB) or A10G provide good throughput-to-cost ratios. Test both with your actual traffic.
  • Models 70B+ parameters: H100 or multi-GPU A100 setups. Consider model quantization (8-bit, 4-bit) to fit larger models on smaller GPUs.

Run benchmarks with your model: measure throughput (tokens/second) and divide hourly instance cost by throughput. The GPU with lowest cost-per-token wins—often not the newest hardware.

3. Implement Autoscaling and Load-Based Provisioning

Static capacity wastes money. Effective autoscaling requires:

  • Metrics-based scaling: Scale on queue depth or GPU utilization, not just CPU metrics
  • Fast cold-start optimization: Container images under 5GB, model weights cached on instance launch
  • Gradual scale-down: 10-15 minute cooldown periods prevent thrashing
  • Scheduled scaling: Pre-scale for known traffic patterns (business hours, batch jobs)

Companies with variable load see 25-40% savings from autoscaling alone.

4. Optimize Batching and Serving Configuration

GPUs process batches far more efficiently than individual requests:

  • Dynamic batching: Accumulate requests for 5-20ms before processing. A 5ms wait enabling batch size 8 can double throughput.
  • Continuous batching: Tools like vLLM and TensorRT-LLM insert new requests into ongoing batches, improving utilization.
  • KV cache optimization: PagedAttention and similar techniques reduce memory waste, allowing larger batches.

Proper batching typically improves cost-efficiency by 30-50% for latency-tolerant workloads.

5. Provider and Region Arbitrage

GPU pricing varies dramatically across providers and regions:

  • Spot/preemptible instances: 50-80% cheaper than on-demand. Viable for batch jobs and with request retry logic.
  • Alternative clouds: CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, Crusoe often 20-40% cheaper than AWS/GCP/Azure for equivalent GPUs.
  • Bare metal providers: For sustained high utilization (70%+), bare metal from providers like Genesis Cloud can be 30-50
